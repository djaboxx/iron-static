# Making Music: Creative Strategies for Electronic Music Producers
*Dennis DeSantis — Ableton, 2015*
*Source: https://makingmusic.ableton.com/*

A collection of 24 patterns (problem/solution pairs) for electronic music producers. Organized into three phases of the creative process. Each pattern names a specific roadblock and gives a concrete instruction to resolve it.

---

## How to Use This

Read around the genre-specific examples — the underlying pattern applies across all electronic music. Patterns refer to each other; following the cross-references is worth it.

---

## Part I — Problems of Beginning

*Can't start. Inspiration is missing. Sound design is eating the idea. Too many options. Fear of the blank session.*

---

### Catalog of Attributes

**Problem:** You're inspired by someone else's music but don't know how to use that inspiration without copying.

**Solution:** Listen carefully — many times — to the source. Catalog its attributes element by element: sound, harmony, melody, rhythm, and form. Write it down concretely but without transcribing. Set the source aside. Use only your catalog as the template for your new work.

A complete catalog looks like this:
> 122 bpm. Drums: 808, four-on-the-floor, filter motion on closed hats. Bass: FM-ish. Electric piano: distorted, dry. Vocals: breathy verses, full-voice choruses. Lead synth: supersaw, enters after second chorus. Harmony: D minor / A major alternating until breakdown, then D major, then E minor / B major. Melody: lots of D, jumps to A, drops to B♭. Rhythm: four-on-the-floor, offbeat eighth bass, metallic hit on "and" of beat 2 every 4 bars. Form: additive layering (16/16/16/32/16/16).

That catalog describes an endless number of different new works. Two musicians reading the same catalog would write completely different songs.

### Active Listening

**Problem:** You listen to a lot of music but don't feel like you're learning from it.

**Solution:** Listen as the primary activity — no other tasks. Then practice listening in three ways:

**Listen in Layers:** Same track, multiple passes, one parameter per pass:
- *Sound* — timbres, texture density, acoustic space, production techniques
- *Harmony* — key, chord progressions, implied harmony if no overt chords
- *Melody* — range, contour, which instrument carries it, melodic vs. implied
- *Rhythm* — how events are distributed within a bar, repeating vs. one-off gestures, which instruments drive time
- *Form* — how sections divide, what defines section boundaries, instrumentation changes over time

**Listen in Chunks:** Load the track into your DAW, match tempo, loop 1–2 bars. Listen to a single parameter in a single chunk as many times as needed, then advance. Gradually widen the loop until you're covering full phrases.

**Listen Subjectively:** What specifically causes your emotional response? Name it. Connect it to a concrete parameter. Once named, you can adapt it.

### Arbitrary Constraints

**Problem:** Infinite options in a DAW make it impossible to choose a starting direction.

*"My freedom thus consists in my moving about within the narrow frame that I have assigned to myself." — Stravinsky*

**Solution:** Apply arbitrary constraints before starting. Narrow the frame of valid options so you can move forward. The constraints are arbitrary — not because bad options need to be removed, but because valid options do. If you constrain yourself wrong, remove the constraint and try again.

**Musical constraints:**
- Make every sound from one sample
- Completely avoid an instrument expected in the genre (Gabriel's *Melt* has no cymbals; Prince's "When Doves Cry" has no bass)
- Restrict harmony to a single chord or a two-chord vamp

**Time constraints:**
- Set a deadline with an external accountability partner
- Schedule specific task types as calendar appointments (e.g., sound design 7–8pm, form 8–9pm, mixing 9–10pm) — treat them like meetings with a client

**Space constraints:**
- Move to a different venue (coffee shop, different room) — you're forced to take only what you can carry

Note: Choosing to work in a specific genre is already an arbitrary constraint. Commit to it.

---

### Presets as Starting Points

**Problem:** You can make music fast using presets, but you're not sure it's "your" music.

**Solution:** Use presets as departure points, not destinations. Find the preset closest to the sound you imagine. Work on the notes and rhythms first. Then come back and tweak the sound until it has your signature.

You don't need to build from init patch. You need to know enough synthesis basics (ADSR, filter cutoff/resonance — six controls at most) to differentiate any preset from any other. That's enough.

The purist vs. assembler spectrum is a false binary. The middle path: let the preset get you started, then earn the sound.

---

### Simple Sounds

**Problem:** You have a melodic or rhythmic idea in your head, but the process of finding the right sound kills the idea.

**Solution:** Start with the simplest possible sound — General MIDI, a plain sine, a basic sawtooth with no effects. Now work exclusively on notes and rhythms. The idea has to be strong enough to stand on its own without impressive sounds carrying it.

Ideas that survive simple sounds are worth building. Ideas that don't are usually the sounds doing the work, not the music.

Acoustic instruments work even better for this — they remove DAW thinking entirely. Set up a mic, record everything.

Note: If your music is fundamentally about sound design and timbre, this technique wastes time. Know which type of producer you are.

---

### Procrastination and Timeboxing

**Problem:** You know you want to make music, but sitting down to do it feels like torture.

**Solution:** Timeboxing. Set a timer for a short, fixed period (20 minutes). Work only on a single defined task during that time — sound design, or drum programming, or arranging — nothing else. Stop when the timer goes off. Take a 5-minute break. Repeat with a different task. After 4–5 rounds, take a longer break.

Why it works: No task is too painful for 20 minutes. The fixed end point prevents the fear of an open-ended commitment.

**Critical:** Stop even when you're in flow. Stopping while it's fun means you end on a high. The memory of the session is positive. Working until frustration sets in does the opposite.

After several days of strict timeboxing, you'll find the procrastination instinct weakens. Gradually extend the timebox. Eventually you won't need it.

---

### Thinking Like an Amateur

**Problem:** Since you got serious about music, you've lost the joy you had when you were just making sounds for yourself.

**Solution:** Remember that "amateur" literally means "lover of." Amateurs have a genuinely more pleasurable experience than professionals because they're free from external pressure.

Try working in a genre where you have no prior track record. Your expectations automatically drop because you have nothing to compare against. Even bad results teach you something.

If you do aspire to make music professionally, compartmentalize. Reserve time for music that exists only for you, with zero commercial intent. That space is where risk-taking lives.

---

### On Work

**Problem:** You're waiting for the flow state that makes music feel effortless. It rarely comes.

*"Genius is one percent inspiration and ninety-nine percent perspiration." — Edison*
*"Inspiration is for amateurs — the rest of us just show up and get to work." — Chuck Close*

**Solution:** The flow state is real but rare and cannot be scheduled. The process of creating art is, fundamentally, work. You will be plagued with doubt, fear, boredom, and disappointment at various points in every project. This is not a sign that something is wrong. It is the normal experience of making music.

Embrace the misery as a structural feature of the process, not a reason to stop. Even the most flow-prone artists get there only after painful effort. Every project, for everyone, requires real work.

## Part II — Problems of Progressing

*You've started. You have material. Now it's stale, you're stuck in a loop, you can't develop it, you don't know how to vary it, the arrangement isn't coming together.*

---

### Fuzzy Boundaries

**Problem:** Your section transitions feel abrupt and mechanical — DAW blocks create hard edges where music should flow.

**Solution:** Break the vertical line between sections. Instead of all tracks cutting simultaneously, apply these techniques across individual tracks:

- **Extend** material from the previous section into the new one (some tracks only)
- **Retract** material early from the previous section before the boundary (some tracks only)
- **Delete** material on either side of the boundary (some tracks only)
- Leave 1–2 tracks unchanged for rhythmic continuity

**Anticipation:** A drum fill that peaks *before* the downbeat, followed by silence and a re-entrance on beat 2. The energy peaks early, creating forward momentum.

**Hesitation:** A drum fill that *extends past* the formal boundary, continuing into the new section. The new material enters underneath the fill, which gradually dissolves.

Both techniques can be applied to any instrument — bass lines, sustained chords, lead lines.

---

### Creating Variation 2: Mutation Over Generations

**Problem:** You have a great 4–8 bar seed idea but can't see how to develop it into a full song.

**Solution:** The one-change rule, applied generationally:

1. Duplicate the original idea
2. Make exactly one meaningful change to the duplicate — one change to sound, harmony, melody, rhythm, or form
3. Duplicate the duplicate (not the original)
4. Make one more meaningful change
5. Repeat 6–8 times

What you end up with: a chain of descendants, each the direct offspring of the previous one, but increasingly distant from the original ancestor. You now have a full palette of related material — different enough to create formal contrast, similar enough to feel like a unified song.

Key: don't go backwards. Each generation inherits all previous changes.

---

### Asynchronous or Polyrhythmic Loops

**Problem:** Your loop-based music sounds static and boring even though your reference tracks make loops feel alive and in motion.

**Solution:** Trigger the same instrument with multiple simultaneous clips of *different lengths* (inter-track MIDI routing required).

Example: A 4-step pattern and a 5-step pattern played together. They drift apart and realign every 20 steps — the ear hears three patterns simultaneously (both originals + their composite), creating apparent complexity from simple components.

Extensions:
- Add a third loop of a third length — composite alignment grows accordingly
- Apply this to automation envelopes (filter cutoff LFO at an odd cycle length against the note loop)
- Use duplicate instruments with slightly altered timbres instead of the exact same instrument
- Offset a loop length by a small non-metric amount for genuine drift (never realigns exactly)
- Even a single "odd" loop against standard-length loops creates interesting effects

Historical context: This is the defining technique of the Berlin School (Tangerine Dream, Klaus Schulze) — multiple modular sequencers, each with a different number of steps. Also: Brian Eno's *Music for Airports* — tape loops of different lengths.

**IRON STATIC application:** This is exactly what the Subharmonicon's four independent sequencer rows are built to do. No DAW routing required — it's physical.

### Programming Beats 2: Linear Drumming

**Problem:** Your drum patterns use instruments in expected roles — hats keep time, kick hits the beats, snare is on 2 and 4. You want more creative patterns but everything you try sounds random.

**Solution:** Linear drumming — monophonic drum programming where no two instruments play simultaneously.

How to think about it: Treat the full drum kit as a single melodic line of notes. Each instrument is equally weighted; no one voice has a timekeeping role, no one voice has an accenting role. The line itself carries the rhythm.

Example structure: Groups of 3 sixteenth notes cycling against a 4/4 grid creates a "rolling" feel — common in drum and bass.

Variation: Fill every sixteenth position with a note, then use velocity variation to create sub-groupings and implied accents within the monophonic line.

Linear patterns read very differently at different tempos. Test at both slow and fast BPMs.

Related: see **Linear Rhythm in Melodies** (hocket) for the melodic equivalent.

### Silence and Noise

**Problem:** You understand sound, harmony, melody, rhythm, and form — but there's still something in the music you love that you can't find in your own work.

**Solution:** Think consciously about what happens between the notes. There are two poles:

**Ultra-clean silence (AtomTM approach):** Each event enters and exits against genuine empty space. No reverb tails, no ambient wash. Everything is short and percussive. The space reads as intentional, not empty.

**Constant noise as texture (Rhythm & Sound / dub techno approach):** Every gap between notes is filled with noise that sounds like old, broken, mysterious equipment. Ways to create this:
- Record an open mic in a quiet room and boost the level dramatically
- Sample the runout groove at the end of a vinyl record
- Process field recordings (urban, factory, nature) with heavy reverb
- Create DAW feedback loops: route a return track output back into itself at a controlled send level, record the result

Treat silence and noise as compositional elements with as much intentionality as any melodic or rhythmic part.

### Creating Melodies 1: Contour

**Problem:** Your melodies feel aimless and forgettable compared to the ones in the music you love.

**Solution:** Control contour — the shape of the melody over time.

**General rules:**
- Balance rise vs. fall: if a melody climbs for a while, it should generally descend by roughly the same amount
- Balance conjunct (stepwise) vs. disjunct (leaps): after a leap, move by step in the opposite direction; after stepwise motion, a leap in the opposite direction is often right
- A good melody has one peak note — the highest pitch occurs only once, usually on a strong beat
- The overall shape is often an arc: start low, climb to the peak at the midpoint, descend in the second half

These rules are not laws — strong melodies break them constantly. But if your melodies feel weak, this is usually why.

### Linear Rhythm in Melodies

**Problem:** You can start a melody but can't generate interesting variations or complexity from it.

**Solution:** Hocket — a compositional technique in which a melody's notes alternate rapidly between two or more instruments so that no two instruments play at the same time.

Recipe:
1. Duplicate the melody to a second track
2. Change the instrument on the second track (similar volume contour; fast attacks work best)
3. Edit both tracks so every note belongs to exactly one track — no note plays on both simultaneously
4. Divide by alternation pattern: every other note, every quarter note, every third sixteenth, etc.

The result: a single melodic idea becomes a cascade of timbres. Even the simplest melody gains complexity through the rapid timbre switching.

Three or more voices are possible — in the extreme, each note can have its own instrument.

**IRON STATIC application:** Hocket between the Take5 and Minibrute2S on the same melodic pattern creates exactly this effect. Different filter characters, same notes, interleaved.

### The Rhythm of Lyrics

**Problem:** Your vocal melodies feel wrong — there's a conflict between the melody and the natural flow of the words.

**Solution:** Scansion — the study of a text's inherent stress patterns. Mark each syllable as strongly (/) or weakly (*) stressed based on natural spoken delivery.

General rules:
- Strong syllables → strong beats (beats 1 and 3 in 4/4; odd subdivisions)
- Weak syllables → weak beats (beats 2 and 4; even subdivisions)
- Stressed syllables: louder, higher in pitch, longer in duration

Placing strong syllables on weak beats and vice versa is not wrong — a lot of contemporary hip-hop uses this deliberately as rhythmic tension. But if your vocal melody feels uncomfortable to sing and uncomfortable to hear, fighting the natural scansion of the lyric is usually the reason.

### Parallel Harmony

**Problem:** You can't replicate the strange, alien-but-coherent quality of chord progressions in deep house, post-dubstep, and related styles.

**Solution:** Parallel harmony — moving from chord to chord by transposing all notes by the same interval in the same direction. No voice leading, no common tones retained, just a copy of the chord moved up or down.

Traditional voice leading would optimize this for minimal hand movement and shared notes between adjacent chords. Parallel harmony ignores these conventions. The result is a chord progression that sounds "wrong" by classical standards but distinctly correct for electronic music — alien and familiar simultaneously.

Two practical techniques:

**Sampled chords:** Sample a single interesting chord from a soul or jazz record. Pitch that one sample up and down. Every transposition preserves the parallel relationship.

**Chord memory:** Use a DAW MIDI chord processor (Ableton's Chord MIDI effect) to add fixed intervals above a single played note. Play a single-note sequence; the device adds the configured intervals to every note, generating parallel harmony automatically.

**IRON STATIC application:** Ableton Chord MIDI FX before any hardware synth. Set Shift1/Shift2/Shift3 to your voicing. Play a mono sequence. Get parallel harmony without programming a single chord in a clip.

### Humanizing With Automation Envelopes

**Problem:** Your electronic instruments sound clinical and mechanical. Live instruments have subtle, constant variation in tuning and note length that makes them feel alive.

**Solution:** Automate envelope and tuning parameters with subtle, small changes over time — not filter sweeps, but ADSR attack and decay times, and micro-pitch variations.

**Amplitude humanization:** Draw slow automation on attack and decay parameters. The goal is barely perceptible change in note length and shape — not a dramatic filter sweep. This can often be achieved via parameter randomization tools rather than long automation envelopes.

**Pitch humanization:** Automate master tuning with tiny variations — a few cents in either direction at most. Larger than a few cents reads as out of tune; smaller reads as alive.

Apply to any parameter, not just envelope and pitch. Most synth parameters are ignored after initial programming. Try subtle automation on unusual parameters — filter resonance, LFO rate, oscillator wave shape — and see what the results offer.

### Deliberately Bad Listening

**Problem:** Your studio monitoring is excellent and your listening is accurate. But sometimes when you stop concentrating and just let the music play, you hear something completely different that seems more interesting.

**Solution:** Listen badly, deliberately. The imperfect listening environment creates acoustic illusions — phantom pitches, unusual bass behavior, unexpected rhythmic artifacts. These illusions can be compositionally useful if you identify them and recreate them in the actual music.

Techniques:
- Move around the room while the music plays (especially corners — bass changes dramatically)
- Listen from outside the room through a wall or doorway (high-frequency content transmits differently than low)
- Take headphones off while the music is playing at high volume — only high-frequency content survives
- Turn the volume down to barely perceptible — ears have unequal frequency sensitivity at low volumes; certain ranges emerge or disappear

Once you find an interesting illusion, figure out how to bake it into the music so normal listeners can hear it.

## Part III — Problems of Finishing

*You have material. You have sections. But the arrangement isn't coming together, the ending won't resolve, and you keep tweaking things that are already done.*

---

### Dramatic Arc

**Problem:** Your arrangements don't feel like journeys. You can't create convincing tension and release over 4–10 minutes.

**Solution:** Structure the arrangement as a dramatic arc (Freytag's pyramid):

1. **Exposition** — introduce the musical materials that will appear throughout: melodic ideas, harmonic progressions, rhythmic gestures
2. **Rising action** — variations and mutations of the exposition materials; tension builds
3. **Climax** — peak of dramatic tension; in electronic music, often marked by a sudden increase in textural density ("the drop")
4. **Falling action** — tension relaxes; mirror of the rising action
5. **Dénouement** — return to the established materials from the exposition, or a gradual dissolution

The dramatic arc can also operate at the micro level (a single melodic phrase with a contour that rises to a peak and descends) and at the macro level (a DJ set over several hours).

Note: dub, minimal techno, and related genres build tension through different means — subtle changes to sound design in an otherwise static texture. The dramatic arc is a model, not a requirement.

**IRON STATIC application:** Tetanus Pulse's 6-section arrangement (Intro → Build → Drop → Breakdown → Climax → Outro) is a direct implementation of this arc.

---

### Arranging as a Subtractive Process

**Problem:** You have too many good ideas but can't build an arrangement. The blank arrangement timeline is paralyzing.

**Solution:** Don't start with an empty timeline. Start by immediately filling the entire timeline with material, as fast as possible — 20 seconds or less. Don't organize, don't think, just paste everything onto every track for the full length of a song.

Now you have a "solid block" of material. The arrangement process becomes sculpture rather than painting — you chip away, create space, remove what doesn't work, rather than adding to emptiness.

Why this works: It's easier to hear what's wrong than to imagine what's right. An overfull arrangement tells you immediately when something clashes, when a section is too long, when an element is too loud.

**Bonus:** Most DAWs have "insert time" and "delete time" commands that shift everything after a point without needing manual cut/paste of every track. Use these constantly when subtracting.

---

### Common Forms 1: Elements of Song Form

**Problem:** You can create ideas but can't organize them into a structure that makes sense.

**Solution:** Understand the vocabulary of standard song sections:

- **Verse (A):** Recurring; 16–32 bars; main body of the song; different lyrics each time in songs with lyrics
- **Chorus (B):** Recurring; contrast to verse; contains the hook; same music *and* lyrics each time; usually appears after the first verse
- **Bridge (C):** Non-recurring; occurs once; substantially different from verse and chorus — different key, unusual harmony, different textural density; often appears after at least one verse and chorus

Standard pop form: **ABABCB** (Verse–Chorus–Verse–Chorus–Bridge–Chorus). This is the majority of contemporary radio pop.

Most underground and experimental electronic music without vocals doesn't use these sections. Instead, formal contrast comes from additive and subtractive layering. But knowing the vocabulary helps you understand what you're choosing to use or subvert.

---

### Unique Events

**Problem:** Your arrangement is structurally solid but still sounds like "loop music" — too predictable to sustain attention after the first listen.

**Solution:** Insert unique events — gestures, sounds, or variations that occur exactly once and never repeat.

Three types:
1. **Single events:** One-shot sounds (pitched or unpitched) placed at a strategic moment. Source: sample library. Effect varies by placement — near formal boundaries reads as transition; mid-phrase in an unexpected location is jarring
2. **Single musical gestures:** One-time variations to otherwise repeating patterns (a note that's different this one time, a rhythm that stutters once, a single extra bar). DAW note transformation tools are useful here
3. **Single processing gestures:** Automation-enabled effect chains that switch on once and then off — a distortion that fires for half a beat, a pitch effect that hits one note out of 64

Use sparingly. Too many unique events creates its own predictability — the listener starts expecting surprises, which defeats the purpose.

---

### Diminishing Returns

**Problem:** The closer you get to finished, the slower you progress. You keep tweaking what's essentially a finished mix, unable to commit.

*"The perfect is the enemy of the good." — Voltaire*
*"Art is never finished, only abandoned." — Leonardo da Vinci*

**Solution:** Learn to identify the point of diminishing returns — where continued work yields meaningless or arbitrary changes rather than genuine improvements.

Signs you've reached it:
- You're making changes that feel significant but sound identical on playback
- You're re-reverting to earlier decisions
- Your initial instinct for a parameter was probably right and you've overridden it twice already

Late-stage tweaking is almost always fear of commitment. Once the song is declared finished, you're accountable for it. Continuing to work defers that accountability but costs you time that could go toward the next song.

At worst, continued tweaking makes the track objectively worse by overriding the correct initial decisions with second-guessed alternatives.

Find the point. Stop there. Start the next one.
